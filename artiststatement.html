Artist Statement

As an immigrant child, I had never considered my status as an American resident and my parents did everything they could for their children to leave those worries up to them. They believed that it wasnâ€™t something for a child to worry about, and I agree. My brother and I were blessed not only by my hard working parents but also with the loving Americans around us who helped us to become naturalized, but we are a rare case. This body of work focuses on aspects of my childhood reflected in the millions of immigrants who emigrated to the United States of America with their parents.

Kore-Americans is a video project focusing on undocumented Korean-American immigrants who are in need of a clean DREAM Act. I aim to focus on individuals and their experiences having to grow up in America as undocumented immigrants alongside American citizens and how the Korean-American Community has aided them, because we are nationwide and a predominantly unspoken minority. This submissiveness and unspoken nature comes out of the culture that we are accustomed to. Although many immigrant children who were brought to America grew up with a mix of cultures and can choose for themselves which parts of each culture that appeals to them, the older generation of immigrants are much harder to persuade.

Asian American immigrants are the fastest growing immigrant populations in the United States, growing by an average of 46 percent in a decade compared to the nationwide average growth of 10 percent during the same decade. Although we are the largest growing immigrant population, we are also the least represented in all of American among the rest of the immigrants. The Korean-American society as a whole is split on the decision to support undocumented Koreans who are trying to live their lives in America. A recent survey was done in 2016 of more than 4,000 Asian Americans, most of whom are foreign born and naturalized. Overall, 58 percent of the people surveyed would support the legislation that would create a path to citizenship for undocumented immigrants in the United States. From those surveyed, a slim majority of Koreans oppose a path to citizenship, while only 41 percent supported it. This response is coming from people who have been in similar situations as undocumented Korean-Americans are facing right now.

The second part of the project, "KOREAN ENOUGH", was brought about by taking the process from the first video and bringing it to diverse but also similar experiences that other Korean-Americans had while growing up with such opposing cultures and environments. Having to act one way at home with a different language and another way outside the house, balancing the different cultures. This complex experience was composed with conversations around Korean food, our comfort food. Korean culture revolves around food and one can learn much about the culture from the cuisine itself.
